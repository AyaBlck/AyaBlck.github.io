Data Quality Assessment

While checking the statistics of the dataset, we noticed that some numerical features had extreme outliers, so we created boxplots to visualise the distribution of these features and identify the outliers that needed to be handled before proceeding with the analysis.

Code 
# columns for boxplots
cols = ['score',
       'age',
       'equity',
       'balance',
       'salary']

# boxplots for numerical features
plt.figure(figsize=(8,7))
for i, col in enumerate(cols):
    sns.boxplot(bank_data[col], color=sns.color_palette('Set3')[i+1], ax=plt.subplot(3, 2, i+1))
    plt.title('Boxplot for {}'.format(col), fontsize=17)
    plt.xlabel(col)
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

As expected, the balance sheet and salary data contain many outliers. The “equity” and “age” features also contain outliers, but they are few and small, so we decided to leave them unchanged. To more accurately determine which data to discard, we calculated the 95th, 97.5, and 99th percentiles. After this, we decided to discard balance and salary data at the 95th percentile.

Key Findings

TipMain Insights
  • Credit score is the strongest churn driver — as scores increase, so does the churn rate
  • Number of products reveals a clear pattern: customers with 1 product have only 10% churn, those with 2 products almost never leave, but customers with 3-4 products churn almost entirely
  • Age shows increasing churn from age 20 to 40, a small spike at 55, and almost no churn after 60
  • Recent activity correlates with churn — likely because customers transfer funds before closing accounts
  • Income and balance — customers with fewer funds are less likely to churn, while those earning above 45,000 show higher churn rates

Results

The analysis revealed several actionable patterns for the bank’s marketing department.

The bank successfully attracts credit and salary-transfer customers, but struggles to retain clients with other products. Customers with 3-4 products are almost entirely churned, suggesting that beyond credit services, the bank’s product offerings may not meet customer expectations.

The data suggests a critical gap: the bank lacks a compelling product for higher-income customers — the “upper middle class” segment earning above the regional average salary.

The relationship between recent activity and churn is particularly interesting. Customers who were recently active are more likely to leave, which makes sense — before closing an account, customers typically transfer their funds to other banks. This activity spike could serve as an early warning signal for the retention team.

WarningRecommendation

The bank should focus on developing premium products for customers with higher incomes (above 45,000) and consider personalised retention strategies for customers aged 30-45, who represent the highest churn risk segment.
